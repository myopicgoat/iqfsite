Analytical pricing: the Black-Scholes formula

The bare minimum

You have seen how to price assets using the discounted expected value under the risk-neutral probability distribution. The part that requires some work is to determine the distribution and compute expectations under it. When assuming log-normality of the returns, this leads to the Black-Scholes model and, in particular, to explicit prices for European options.

Getting to the Black-Scholes model is actually quite straightforward but does require a little bit of maths. Ignoring most of the maths here (full details are available in the appendix ()) the main steps are:

  1. model the log-returns \(\log (S_{T}/S_{t})\) as \(\mathcal N(\mu \tau,\sigma^{2} \tau)\) (in agreement with our previous observations),

  2. in the risk neutral world, all assets must be expected to return the bank account rate \(r\) hence the distribution must be modified in the following way: \(\mu\leftarrow \mu^{\star} = r-\sigma^{2}/2\) so that the pricing formula holds for the asset: \(S_t = \Phi_r(t,T) \mathbb E^{\star}_{t}[ S_{T}\)], (\(\dagger\))

  3. the previous two points imply a specific model for the distribution of \(S_{T}\),

  4. European derivatives can then be priced by computing expected values under that distribution model.

\(\dagger\) here you need to recall that the expected value of a \(\mathrm{LogNormal}(m,s^{2})\) is \(\exp(m+s^{2}/2)\).

In particular, for the European long call, the expected value is not too hard to compute and leads to following pricing formula:

\[\begin{equation} V_t^{\text{call}} = S_t \Phi(d_1) - K\exp(-r\tau)\Phi(d_2) \end{equation}\]

where \(\Phi(a)=\int_{-\infty}^a \exp(-x^2/2)/\sqrt{2\pi}\mathrm{d}x\) (which can be computed using the norm.cdf function from scipy.stats).

Remark: do not confuse the symbol \(\Phi(a)\), the integral above and \(\Phi_r(t,T)\) the discounting factor \(\exp(-r(T-t))\). Here you will only see this symbol in the context of the Black-Scholes formula so there should not be any ambiguity. In textbooks they sometimes write \(\mathrm{CND}(a)\) (Cumulative Normal Distribution) instead of \(\Phi(a)\).

The constants \(d_{1,2}\) are given by

\[\begin{equation} d_{1,2} = {\log(S_t/K)+(r\pm \sigma^2/2)\tau\over\sigma \sqrt{\tau}} \end{equation}\]

A more detailed development is available in the appendix if you are interested. In the mean time, you can easily write a code that computes that value:

def callPriceBS(St,K,tau,r,v):
    d1 = ( (np.log(St/K)) + (r+np.power(v,2)/2)*tau ) / (v*np.sqrt(tau))
    d2 = d1-v*np.sqrt(tau)
    return St*norm.cdf(d1)-K*np.exp(-r*tau)*norm.cdf(d2)

So for example the price of a call which expires in 2 years, with a strike price of £100, a current asset price of £105, an annual interest rate of 5% and a volatility of 15% is

callPriceBS(105,100,2,0.05,0.15)

which is approximately £17.4.

Greeks

When considering adding specific derivatives to their portfolio, traders will often consider specific numerical quantities which help characterise these derivatives and, by extension, their portfolio. These numerical quantities are known as the "Greeks"; a set of symbols (most of which actually are Greek letters) representing the sensitivity of the current value (price) of the derivative with respect to small changes of parameters.

It should be stressed here that the Greeks form an essential set of tools for the traders, in a way even more so than the price itself. They tell them what their risks are subject to variations in the market when investing in a particular derivative.

The essential: Delta, Vega, Theta

Delta

The Delta (\(\Delta\)) of a derivative quantifies the sensitivity of the value of the derivative considered to a small change in the price of the underlying:

\[\begin{equation} \Delta := {\partial V \over\partial S} \approx {V(S_t+\Delta S)-V(S_t)\over \Delta S} \end{equation}\]

where \(\Delta S\) is a small change in the underlying (which may be positive or negative). In words, this letter quantifies how much the value of the derivative changes (\(\Delta V = V(S_t+\Delta S)-V(S_t)\)) when the value of the underlying changes a little bit (\(\Delta S\)).

As an aside, you may recognise the first term which is the partial derivative of the price of the derivative with respect to that of the underlying. It is defined as the limit of the right-hand side when the perturbation \(\Delta S\) goes to zero.

Note also that this letter is computed at a specific time \(t\) and will therefore vary with time.

In the case of the Call, you have seen before an explicit formula for the price and you can compute the Delta exactly (analytically) provided you are comfortable with differentiation (it actually all boils down to \(\Phi(d_1)\)). If not or, in general, if you don’t have an explicit formula for the price, you can also estimate the Delta numerically:

def callDeltaBS(St,K,tau,r,v):
    d1 = ( (np.log(St/K)) + (r+np.power(v,2)/2)*tau ) / (v*np.sqrt(tau))
    return norm.cdf(d1)

def callDeltaNumerical(St,K,tau,r,v):
    deltaS = St/1000
    Vplus  = callPriceBS(St+deltaS,K,tau,r,v)
    V      = callPriceBS(St,K,tau,r,v)
    return (Vplus-V)/deltaS

trying both out with realistic values you can see that the numerical approximation can be perfectly acceptable (here within half a percent):

dAnalytical = callDeltaBS(60,75,2,0.05,0.1)
dNumerical  = callDeltaNumerical(60,75,2,0.05,0.1)
pctDiff     = np.abs(dAnalytical-dNumerical)/dAnalytical*100

print("Delta (Analytical): ", dAnalytical)
print("Delta (Numerical) : ", dNumerical)
print("Percentage diff   : ", pctDiff)

Vega

The Vega (\(\nu\) so actually "nu" but known as "Vega", probably because it looks like a "V", maybe also because it rhymes with other letters…​) quantifies the sensitivity to the volatility \(\sigma\) of the underlying:

\[\begin{equation} \nu = {\partial V\over \partial \sigma} \approx {V(\sigma_t + \Delta \sigma)-V(\sigma_t)\over \Delta \sigma} \end{equation}\]

where \(\sigma_{t}\) denotes the current estimate of the volatility.

Theta

Finally, the Theta (\(\Theta\)) quantifies the sensitivity to the life \(\tau=(T-t)\) of the contract. In this case:

\[\begin{equation} \Theta = {\partial V\over \partial \tau} \approx {V(\tau+\Delta\tau)-V(\tau)\over \Delta\tau} \end{equation}\]

Note that, assuming we can compute these for a single derivative, we can also compute these for an entire portfolio. The extension is very simple, we just need to add them together. For example, you may have two identical calls and one put in your portfolio, then the Delta of the portfolio is given by

\[\begin{equation} \Delta^{\text{portf.}} = 2\Delta^{\text{call}}+\Delta^{\text{put}}. \end{equation}\]

(Bonus): Other greeks

There are a few other letters that are of interest to the traders like the Rho (sensitivity to interest rate). The three seen so far (Delta, Vega, Theta) and Rho are first order letters in that they are directly interested in the variation of the price of the derivative.

It is also sometimes of interest to compute higher order letters quantifying the sensitivity of another letter to a parameter. An important one is the Gamma which measures the sensitivity of the Delta to the price of the underlying or:

\[\begin{eqnarray} \Gamma = {\partial^2 V\over \partial S^2} &\approx& {\left(V(S_t+\Delta S) - V(S_t)\right) - \left(V(S_t)-V(S_t-\Delta S)\right)\over \Delta S^2}. \end{eqnarray}\]

(Bonus): Hedging with the Greeks

Although this does not aim to be a course on trading, it is helpful to understand some of the ways traders use the Greeks to build their portfolio and therefore why being able to compute them accurately is important.

Greeks, as you have seen, fundamentally represent risks attached to a specific asset. A trader may seek to reduce those risks by having a portfolio with one or several Greeks at or around zero. For example, traders may consider Delta-neutral portfolios (with a \(\Delta\) (approximately) equal equal to zero) and seek to, at any given time, adjust (balance) their portfolio such that this holds.

Holding such "hedges" perfectly is usually impossible and costly. Indeed, at every time step, it requires buying and selling assets such that the portfolio meets the requirements but, typically, these operations incur transaction fees. This makes repeated "rebalancing" of a portfolio costly. A trader therefore has to make a tradeoff between frequent rebalancing and sticking to a strategy.

The volatility smile

Let us come back now to the Black-Scholes formula proper. The only non-observable parameter of the formula in the current context is the volatility which we currently assume is modelled based on observations:

However, on the market, we can assume that there already exists lots of calls and puts being sold at some price. We can therefore define the concept of implied volatility by reversing the second arrow (going from Prices to Volatility).

Definition

The implied volatility of a call/put is the volatility such that, when used in the Black-Scholes formula, we recover the same price as the one observed on the market.

We could therefore also consider using the implied volatility to calibrate our model? Intuitively, it would be nice if both the volatility modelled on the log-returns and the implied volatility matched. However, as you will see, this is not the case. This section is dedicated to understanding why and explaining how to work around it.

Observing the implied volatility

On markets with lots of trades, there are lots of options available with all sorts of strikes and maturities and for every asset. If we consider calls and puts on AAPL with a fixed maturity for example:

Implied volatilities corresponding to the prices of the AAPL options (data: courtesy of IMC)

So as you can see, the implied volatility varies (quite a lot!) with the strike. However all these puts and calls are on the same asset and with the same maturity, according to our model there should therefore only be a single volatility \(\sigma\) corresponding to the log-returns?

Understanding the issue

The phenomenon identified above is known as the volatility smile and its cause is to be found in the LogNormal model itself. You will remember that when observing the log-returns, a normal distribution had been fitted to it and that the fit was not perfect. Had it been a very good fit, the implied volatility observed would be roughly constant (corresponding to \(\sigma\)). But here the implied volatility is clearly not constant!

We had stuck to the LogNormal model because it has the advantage of being simple to work with (and yields the Black-Scholes formula). But now the limitations appear rather clearly.

To work around this issue, it would be natural to suggest fitting another model (for example the Student-t) or even more complex models (stochastic volatility models, jump models, …​). However, the approach most often used by practitioner is simple and allows us to stay in the same framework as before.

(Bonus): Working with the volatility smile

So far we started from the LogNormal model and deduced the price of options implied by that model. We have now observed that the implied volatility is not constant. So we could suggest still using the same formula but with a volatility that is a function of the strike: \(\sigma(K)\). Even further, the smile above also changes if the price of the underlying changes. Therefore it is common to suggest a volatility model of the form \(\sigma(f(S,K))\) where a simple common choice of \(f\) is

\[\begin{equation} f(S,K) = \log {S\exp(r\tau)\over K}. \end{equation}\]

This is a pragmatic approach which allows both to keep using the Black-Scholes formula (easy to implement, and fast to compute and also directly yielding Greeks), and to better fit the underlying financial reality. The latter allows to price more accurately in parameter domains where there is no market data.

In state of the art models, people consider some simple parametrized function for \(\sigma\), typically a combination of polynomials, and fit it to the implied volatility smile.
