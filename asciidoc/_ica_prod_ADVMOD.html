Advanced Models

The trinomial tree

The trinomial tree is a simple extension of the binomial tree where, at each node, you can go up (factor \(u\)), down (factor \(d\)) or stay at the same price. In order for the tree to be recombining, we can pose \(u=1/d\). The tree then looks like:

Illustration of a recombining trinomial tree

The main advantage of the trinomial tree over the binomial tree is that it is a more accurate and more flexible model and can exhibit better numerical properties. In particular, there are many ways in which the trinomial tree can be parametrised which are consistent with the same LogNormal model and this can be useful when calibrating the tree with a specific purpose in mind such as the pricing of a given derivative.

Pricing in the trinomial tree

Pricing is done in much the same way as in the binomial tree, the main parameter that is modified is the risk-neutral probability. Indeed, there are now three branches to consider at each node and hence three probabilities.
If you write \(p^{\star}_{u}\) the probability of going up, \(p^{\star}_{d}\) the probability of going down then the probability of staying at the same level must be \(p^{\star}_{m}=1-(p^{\star}_{u}+p^{\star}_{d})\). Now, as opposed to the binomial tree where looking at a single step immediately yielded the risk-neutral probability, here you need to look at two layers in order to get \(p^{\star}_{u}\) and \(p^{\star}_{d}\). The key is still to verify the principle of risk-neutral pricing. Over the first layer this gives:

\[\begin{equation} S_0 = \Phi_r S_0(up^\star_u + p^\star_m + dp^\star_d) \end{equation}\]

where \(\Phi_{r} = \Phi_{r}(0,\Delta t) = \exp(-r\Delta t)\). Rearranging leads to

\[\begin{equation} \Phi_r^{-1} = up^\star_u + p^\star_m + dp^\star_d. \end{equation}\]

Following the same logic, the second layer gives:

\[\begin{equation} \Phi_r^{-2} = u^{2}p^{\star2}_{u} + 2u p^{\star}_{u}p^{\star}_{m} + 2p^{\star}_{u}p^{\star}_{d}+ p^{\star2}_{m}+2dp^{\star}_{m}p^{\star}_{d} + d^{2} p^{\star2}_{d}. \end{equation}\]

and you can check that the solution to these two equations is given by

\[\begin{equation} p^{\star}_{u} = \left({\sqrt{\Psi_{r}}-\sqrt{d}\over \sqrt{u}-\sqrt{d}}\right)^{2},\quad\text{and}\quad p^{\star}_{d} = \left({\sqrt{u}-\sqrt{\Psi_{r}}\over \sqrt{u}-\sqrt{d}}\right)^{2}. \end{equation}\]

(recall that \(\Psi_r=\Phi_r^{-1}\)). As for the binomial model, you want the trinomial model to converge to the same LogNormal model and for this, \(u\) and \(d\) can be set to

\[\begin{equation} u,d = \exp ( \pm \sigma \sqrt{2\Delta t}) \end{equation}\]

It is a good exercise to code this and compare the performances with respect to the binomial tree against the analytical Black Scholes for the pricing of a call for example.

Other parametrisation

The parametrisation described above is not the only way of parametrising the tree which is compatible with the LogNormal model and different choices may be favourable to specific contexts. For example, in the Kamrad and Ritchen parametrisation, an added stretch parameter \(\lambda\ge1\) is considered with

\[\begin{equation} u,d = \exp(\pm\lambda\sigma\sqrt{2\Delta t}). \end{equation}\]

This \(\lambda\) can then be chosen such that exact values are attained in the tree which improves the accuracy of the pricing when considering derivatives such as the barrier option. This is however beyond the scope of this course, we refer to XXXXX for more informations.

Towards more advanced models

Replicating the trinomial tree

So far we have always considered that you are computing the value of a derivative at the same time as you are buying it and hence you know the initial (spot) price of the underlying asset. However, it may be interesting to pre-compute the tree in which case the price of the underlying asset when the contract is instantiated is unknown. You may also want to compute the \(\Delta\) of a derivative which, as you may remember, is given by the variation in derivative price over a variation in the price of the underlying.
To simplify the presentations, say that we model the initial price of an underlying with three possible values: \(S_{0},uS_{0}\) and \(dS_{0}\). With this convenient model choice, we do not need to compute one tree for each of those three initial prices since they interlace:

The pricing process remains the same (and so does the parametrisation), you will just get three prices for the derivative corresponding to the different initial prices of the underlying asset. In particular, this directly gives you a way to look at the \(\Delta\) of the derivative around \(S_{0}\).

From tree pricing to grid pricing

The replication discussed above should be reasonably intuitive to understand. Now, imagine we do this replication many times and over many steps. Then, ignoring the edges, you will get a grid which, when going backward through it to perform the pricing, looks mostly like

where \(i\) indexes the rows and \(j\) the columns (steps). As before, \(V_{i,j}\) is given by arbitrage-free pricing. Denote \(\Phi_{r}=\exp(-r\Delta t)\) where \(N\) is the number of steps and let \(\alpha=p^{\star}_{u}\Phi_{r}\), \(\beta=p^{\star}_{m}\Phi_{r}\) and \(\gamma=p^{\star}_{d}\Phi_{r}\) then we must have:

\[\begin{equation} V_{i,j} = \alpha V_{i+1,j+1} + \beta V_{i,j+1} + \gamma V_{i-1,j+1}. \end{equation}\]

Now if linear algebra doesn’t scare you, we can actually represent this operation as a matrix-vector product where the matrix is tridiagonal:

\[\begin{equation} V_{:,j} = \left[\begin{array}{ccccccc} & &\ddots & & &&&\\ & &\alpha & \beta & \gamma && &\\ & & & \alpha & \beta & \gamma &&\\ & & & & \alpha & \beta & \gamma& \\ & & & & & &\ddots& \end{array} \right] V_{:,j+1} \end{equation}\]

which is a convenient way of computing the columns of the grid from right (final time) to left (initial time). The colon (:) indicates that we consider the entire column of values i.e., all \(V_{i,j}\) for a fixed \(j\). More interestingly, you may recognise the particular form of a finite-difference solver. If you do, the next point may be of interest to you.

The Black-Scholes PDE

If you are familiar with partial differential equations (PDE) and their numerical solutions, then you may have recognised the form of the scheme at the previous point as a finite-difference solver for a second order PDE. Indeed, it can be shown (although this is not in the scope of this course) that pricing a European derivative as we have done before under the LogNormal modelling assumption is equivalent to solving the Black-Scholes PDE:

\[\begin{equation} {\partial V(S,t)\over \partial t} + r S {\partial V(S,t)\over \partial S} + \frac12 {\sigma^{2}S^{2}}{\partial^{2}V(S,t)\over \partial S^{2}} - rV(S,t) = 0 \end{equation}\]

where \(V\) is the value of the derivative. The boundary conditions are:

  • terminal condition: \(V(.,T)\)=payoff of the derivative,

  • left-boundary (for a call): \(\lim_{S\to 0}V(S,t)= 0\),

  • right-boundary (for a call): \(\lim_{S\to \infty}V(S,t)=S-K\exp(-r(T-t))\).

  • domain, \(S>0\), \(t\in [0,T\)].

Looking at it from this angle opens up the way to using many well-known numerical schemes such as finite-difference and finite-element methods. These algorithms have been very well studied in other engineering contexts (e.g., in fluid mechanics) and are very well understood which is something we can leverage.

The need for performance computing

One can separate the trading in options into roughly two segments, one is the so called OTC market (Over The Counter) and the other is the screen trading on electronic exchanges.

In the OTC market there are typically few transaction but with big notional value (value of the leveraged position). These transactions can contain many derivatives and amongst these very exotic options, often with special features for just one customer. Valuing these trades is complex. But as these transactions are infrequent, it is possible to spend days on their valuation.

Screen trading on the electronic exchanges is a completely different game. There you deal with standardised vanilla options, mostly European or American style. Trading is highly automated, very fast and typically low in volume. Your option price calculation therefore needs to be very fast in order to be competitive.

We have seen that European options can be calculated using a closed form analytical expression, while for American options we need to use a numerical scheme. Of the parameters that the option price depends on, the price of the underlying and is one of the fastest changing ones. If you had to recompute a tree for every new value of the underlying you would be too slow and not competitive. By using wider trees, finite difference schemes and interpolating values you can avoid some of the re-computations: the upfront computational cost is larger but once you solved the Black-Scholes PDE, you can quickly price the derivative for basically all prices of the underlying using interpolation.

However, you saw the issue of the volatility smile. Not only does the underlying price change but also the volatility which is coupled to it. Let’s say you solved the PDE for an underlying price \(S = 100\) and volatility \(\sigma_{100} = 20\%\) and that the price then moves to to \(S′ = 101\) while the parameterised volatility moves to \(\sigma_{101} = 19%\). You need to solve the PDE again! Yet all is not lost, you can expect that the price might jump down again to \(100\) but it is smart to remember previous calculations in order to avoid doing the same work twice. Doing this is called caching results.

Coming up with smart schemes for caching results and interpolating between them is the way to keep up with fast markets and be able to be the first to react to changing market conditions.

Discussion

In this section you saw that it is easy to extend the simple binomial model and that, based on the application of interest, a specific model such as the trinomial model may be preferred. There are many more numerical models out there, each of which with their advantages for pricing specific derivatives or for recovering specific properties.
